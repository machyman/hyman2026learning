# What to assign, and when

*The adoption quickstart: a one-page schedule for instructors.*

Students will not go looking for the student guide. AI is free and takes no effort to start using,
and ease crowds out intention. A student who has never been asked to think about how to use AI well
has no reason to seek out a guide on the subject. You are its only reliable route.

Everything listed below already exists. Nothing here asks you to write anything.

## If you assign only three things

1. `students/start-here.md`, in week one.
2. `companion/spp/`, before the first assignment where AI is permitted.
3. `students/checklist.md`, before they submit that assignment.

Ten minutes, two minutes, three minutes. That is the whole minimum, and it is enough to move a
student from fumbling to deliberate.

## The full schedule

| When | Give them | Their time | What it does |
|---|---|---|---|
| Week 1 | `students/start-here.md` | 10 min | The on-ramp. What AI is good and bad for, and the one rule: try to solve. |
| Week 1 | `companion/dpp/` | 10 min, once | They configure their AI's persistent settings. A one-time setup that improves every conversation after it. |
| Before the first AI-permitted assignment | `companion/spp/` | 2 min | A paste-in prompt. It turns an answer machine into a study partner that asks what they have already tried. |
| Before the first AI-permitted assignment | Your AI-use category, in writing | none | Say which of the four categories applies. `instructors/briefings.md` has three ready-to-drop syllabus policies; `instructors/assignment-templates.md` has per-assignment language. |
| Week 1, if you want a signed agreement | `companion/compact/` | 5 min | The Student Compact, in signable form. Some courses use it, some do not. |
| First time they work a real problem with AI | A study session from `students/study-sessions/` | 15 min | A worked walkthrough in statistics, programming, physics, or mathematics. Pick the one that matches your course. |
| Mid-term, if they are drifting | `students/from-answers-to-learning.md` | 10 min | For the student who is extracting answers and not learning. |
| Before they submit | `students/checklist.md` | 3 min | Ten items. The last is the blank-page test, and it is the strongest one on the list. |
| Before they submit, if you require disclosure | `students/disclosure-templates.md` | 5 min | Templates. They fill one in and attach it. |
| If your policy says you may ask to see the AI thread | `students/sharing-ai-conversations.md` | 5 min | How to share a conversation with you. All three syllabus policies in `briefings.md` reserve this right, so students need to know how. |

## For you, not for them

| Read | When |
|---|---|
| `instructors/first-time-guide.md` | Before the term, if you have not taught with AI before. Start here. |
| `instructors/briefings.md` | When you write the syllabus. Three ready-to-drop AI policies: restrictive, default, permissive. |
| `instructors/assignment-templates.md` | When you write an assignment. Reusable patterns that build in verification. |
| `instructors/rubrics/` | When you set up grading. |
| `instructors/assessment-models.md` | When class size is your binding constraint. |
| `instructors/faculty-faq.md` | When a colleague asks you something you cannot answer. |
| `instructors/semester-redesign.md` | When you are ready to rebuild a course rather than patch one. |

## Make it stick

Students treat an optional reading as optional. Tie each one to something that has a date on it.

- Tie the setup to a deadline. "Bring a configured AI to Friday's lab."
- Tie the checklist to the submission. Make it a required attachment or a line on the cover sheet.
- Tie the study session to the first assignment where AI is allowed, not to a week with nothing due.

A reading with no hook is a reading nobody does. That is as true of this page as of any other.
